What Mold Actually Does to Indoor Air

Mold is a living organism that reproduces by releasing spores ‚Äî tiny particles small enough to remain suspended in the air for hours. A single mold colony can release thousands of spores per minute. These spores, along with mycotoxins (toxic compounds some mold species produce) and microbial volatile organic compounds (mVOCs) (the source of that musty smell), all become part of your indoor air.

The result is air that is biologically contaminated ‚Äî even when the space looks and smells clean. Indoor air quality studies consistently show that the air inside homes and buildings with active mold growth can contain spore concentrations 10 to 1,000 times higher than outdoor air.

Health Effects of Mold Exposure

Mold exposure affects people differently depending on the type of mold, the concentration of spores, the duration of exposure, and individual sensitivity. Here is what the research shows:

Respiratory Symptoms

The most common health effects from mold exposure are respiratory. Spores inhaled into the lungs trigger inflammatory responses that can cause:

  • Coughing, wheezing, and shortness of breath
  • Nasal and sinus congestion
  • Throat irritation and postnasal drip
  • Worsening of asthma ‚Äî including new-onset asthma in previously healthy individuals
  • Hypersensitivity pneumonitis (an inflammatory lung condition from repeated spore exposure)

Allergic Reactions

Many mold species are potent allergens. People with mold allergies experience immune reactions including sneezing, itchy or watery eyes, skin rashes, and hives. Importantly, these reactions often occur indoors year-round ‚Äî unlike seasonal pollen allergies ‚Äî making mold exposure easy to mistake for a chronic allergy condition.

Neurological and Cognitive Symptoms

Mycotoxin-producing mold species ‚Äî such as Stachybotrys chartarum (commonly called "black mold") ‚Äî have been associated in some studies with neurological symptoms including headaches, difficulty concentrating, memory problems, and fatigue. Research in this area is ongoing, but the connection between chronic mycotoxin exposure and cognitive symptoms is increasingly recognized.

"If your allergy symptoms get better when you leave your home or office and return when you come back, mold in the building may be the cause ‚Äî not seasonal pollen."

Who Is Most at Risk?

While anyone can be affected by mold exposure, certain groups face significantly greater health risks:

  • Infants and young children ‚Äî developing respiratory systems are more vulnerable to spore damage
  • Elderly individuals ‚Äî reduced immune function makes fighting off mold-related infections harder
  • People with asthma or allergies ‚Äî mold is a direct trigger for both conditions
  • Immunocompromised individuals ‚Äî those undergoing chemotherapy, organ transplant recipients, or people with HIV/AIDS can develop serious mold infections
  • People with chronic lung conditions ‚Äî including COPD and cystic fibrosis

  • Symptoms that began or worsened after moving to a new home, office, or after water damage
  • Persistent fatigue or brain fog with no identified medical cause

The Most Dangerous Mold Species

Not all mold is equally hazardous, but several species commonly found in Washington, DC homes warrant particular attention:

  • Stachybotrys chartarum (black mold) ‚Äî a mycotoxin-producing species that thrives on wet drywall and wood. Requires sustained moisture and is associated with severe respiratory and neurological symptoms.
  • Aspergillus ‚Äî one of the most common indoor mold genera; certain species produce aflatoxins and can cause serious lung infections in immunocompromised individuals.
  • Cladosporium ‚Äî a very common allergen found on window frames, HVAC systems, and bathroom surfaces throughout the DC area.
  • Penicillium ‚Äî thrives in water-damaged buildings and is a potent allergen and asthma trigger.
