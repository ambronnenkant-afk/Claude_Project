Mold in DC Row Homes:
Why Older Housing Is Especially Vulnerable

By MoldCure¬∑ February 25, 2026¬∑ üïê 6 min read¬∑ Washington, DC

Washington, DC's iconic row homes ‚Äî the brick-fronted Federal and Victorian townhouses lining streets from Capitol Hill to Petworth ‚Äî are among the most sought-after properties in the region. They're also among the most mold-prone. A combination of age, construction methods, the city's swampy climate, and the structural quirks inherent to attached housing creates conditions that make mold an ever-present risk for DC row home owners.

Why DC's Housing Stock Is Uniquely Vulnerable

More than 60% of Washington DC's residential housing was built before 1960. Much of the most desirable stock ‚Äî in neighborhoods like Capitol Hill, Georgetown, Adams Morgan, Bloomingdale, and Petworth ‚Äî dates to the late 1800s and early 1900s. These homes were built before modern moisture-control standards, vapor barriers, and mechanical ventilation existed as concepts.

The result is housing that, despite its beauty and charm, was never designed to handle the moisture challenges of a 21st-century DC summer ‚Äî and often lacks the structural features that prevent the moisture accumulation mold needs to grow.

"A 120-year-old row home was built before vapor barriers, modern insulation, or mechanical ventilation existed. It was never designed for DC's modern humidity levels ‚Äî or modern central air conditioning creating condensation inside walls."

The Six Biggest Mold Risk Factors in DC Row Homes

üß± Solid Masonry Walls Without Vapor Barriers

Most pre-1940 DC row homes are built with solid brick or stone walls ‚Äî no insulation cavity, no vapor barrier. Moisture from outside migrates directly through the masonry, condensing on interior surfaces and creating ideal conditions for mold growth inside walls.

ü™ü Original Single-Pane Windows

Many historic row homes retain original wood-frame single-pane windows. These create significant cold surfaces in winter that collect condensation ‚Äî a chronic moisture source on sills, frames, and surrounding walls that fuels mold growth year after year.

üèöÔ∏è English Basements and Semi-Below-Grade Spaces

The classic DC English basement ‚Äî a semi-subterranean level used as a rental unit or storage ‚Äî sits below or at grade, directly adjacent to soil. Without proper waterproofing, these spaces receive moisture from ground infiltration, particularly after the heavy rain events common in DC.

üîß Aging Plumbing in Walls and Floors

Homes built in the early 1900s often have original or early-replacement galvanized steel or cast-iron pipes that have been corroding for decades. Slow, hidden leaks inside walls ‚Äî dripping for months or years ‚Äî create the chronic moisture that produces extensive mold colonies before any visible sign appears.

üí® Limited Natural Ventilation

Row homes share two side walls with neighbors, meaning windows exist only on the front and rear. This limits cross-ventilation significantly ‚Äî and in summer, when DC humidity regularly exceeds 70%, poor air movement means moisture stays trapped inside, accelerating mold growth on any cool surface.

‚ùÑÔ∏è Modern AC Creating New Condensation Points

Ironically, retrofitting central air conditioning into an old row home creates new mold risks. Cold supply ducts running through warm, humid wall and ceiling spaces sweat condensation. Improperly sealed duct penetrations allow humid air into wall cavities where it condenses on cool surfaces ‚Äî a problem unknown in homes built in the era of natural ventilation.

The Freeze-Thaw Factor: A DC-Specific Problem

Washington DC's winters ‚Äî with temperatures regularly cycling above and below freezing multiple times per season ‚Äî are particularly hard on old masonry. As water in brick and mortar freezes and expands, then thaws, it gradually opens micro-cracks in the masonry that accelerate moisture infiltration. Over decades, this process can compromise the moisture integrity of an entire wall assembly, creating pervasive damp conditions that make mold control very difficult without professional remediation.

DC Neighborhoods With the Highest Mold Risk

While mold can occur anywhere in the city, certain DC neighborhoods see disproportionately high rates of mold issues based on housing age, basement prevalence, and proximity to lower-lying areas with historically higher groundwater tables:

  • Bloomingdale and Eckington ‚Äî historically flood-prone areas; many homes have experienced repeated basement flooding
  • Capitol Hill ‚Äî dense concentration of very old row homes with English basements frequently used as rental units
  • Georgetown ‚Äî some of DC's oldest housing stock, including homes predating the Civil War
  • Adams Morgan and Columbia Heights ‚Äî high density of Victorian-era row homes with limited ventilation and many converted multi-unit buildings
  • Petworth and Brightwood ‚Äî large inventory of early 20th-century homes, many with unfinished or minimally finished basements

üîç Signs Your DC Row Home May Have a Mold Problem

  • Musty or earthy smell in basement, bathroom, or rear of the house
  • Efflorescence (white chalky deposits) on basement walls ‚Äî a sign of water infiltration
  • Paint bubbling, peeling, or staining on exterior-facing walls
  • Condensation on walls or windows throughout winter
  • Dark staining around window frames or sills
  • Wood floors or baseboards warping or cupping without explanation
  • History of basement flooding, even minor or years ago

What to Do If You Suspect Mold in Your Row Home

The biggest mistake DC row home owners make is attempting to treat visible mold with surface cleaners or bleach without addressing the underlying moisture source or the hidden contamination that has likely spread beyond the visible colony. In a solid masonry building with limited ventilation, mold can colonize wall cavities extensively before any signs appear on finished surfaces.

The right approach:

  1. Professional inspection first. Have a professional assess moisture entry points, inspect likely problem areas (basement walls, window frames, bathroom walls, kitchen areas near plumbing), and test air quality before any treatment.
  2. Identify and fix the moisture source. No remediation will be permanent without addressing the water source ‚Äî whether that's a plumbing leak, failed basement waterproofing, window frame failures, or HVAC condensation.
  3. Treat the whole building. In an attached row home, mold spores spread through every space the air reaches. Whole-building dry-fog treatment ensures no spores are left to recolonize treated areas.
  4. Ongoing moisture management. Use a dehumidifier to maintain indoor humidity below 50%, ensure bathroom exhaust fans vent outside (not into attic spaces), and monitor known problem areas seasonally.
