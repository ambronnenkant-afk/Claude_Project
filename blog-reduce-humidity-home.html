How to Reduce Humidity in Your Home
This Summer

By MoldCure¬∑ February 25, 2026¬∑ üïê 4 min read¬∑ Washington, DC

Washington, DC summers are notoriously hot and humid ‚Äî with average outdoor relative humidity regularly exceeding 70% from June through September. While you can't control the weather outside, you can control your indoor environment. And that matters enormously, because humidity above 60% indoors is all mold needs to grow. Here's a practical guide to keeping indoor humidity under control and protecting your home all summer long.

Why Humidity Is the #1 Mold Driver

Mold does not need a water leak or visible moisture to grow. All it needs is relative humidity above approximately 60% sustained over time ‚Äî and organic material to grow on, which every home provides in abundance (drywall, wood framing, carpet, ceiling tiles, grout). In DC summers, achieving and maintaining indoor humidity at safe levels without active management is essentially impossible.

The math is stark: at 70% relative humidity ‚Äî typical for a DC summer day ‚Äî any surface that is slightly cooler than the ambient air (a north-facing wall, the back of a closet, a bathroom ceiling) can collect moisture. Given enough time, that moisture feeds mold.

8 Practical Ways to Reduce Indoor Humidity This Summer

  • 1

    Run a Whole-Home or Portable Dehumidifier

    This is the single most effective intervention. A whole-home dehumidifier integrated into your HVAC system maintains target humidity levels throughout the house automatically. For renters or smaller spaces, portable units are effective for individual rooms ‚Äî prioritize basements, bathrooms, and bedrooms. Target 45‚Äì50% relative humidity.

  • 2

    Don't Vent Your AC Higher Than 74¬∞F

    Your central air conditioning removes humidity from the air as it cools it. The lower you run it, the more moisture it extracts. Setting your thermostat above 76¬∞F in a DC summer means your AC runs less ‚Äî and extracts less moisture ‚Äî allowing indoor humidity to climb even with the AC running.

  • 3

    Always Use Exhaust Fans in Bathrooms and Kitchens

    A 10-minute shower adds a significant amount of water vapor to your indoor air. Run your bathroom exhaust fan during every shower and for at least 20 minutes afterward. For kitchen cooking, use your range hood. Make sure both fans actually vent to the exterior ‚Äî not into your attic or wall cavity, which is a common installation mistake in DC row homes.

  • 4

    Keep Interior Doors Open

    Closed closet and bedroom doors trap humid air in spaces that receive little airflow and no direct AC cooling. Keep interior doors open to allow conditioned, dehumidified air to circulate throughout all spaces ‚Äî including closets, which are among the most common locations for mold growth in DC homes.

  • 5

    Don't Air-Dry Laundry Indoors

    A full load of laundry air-dried indoors can release over a gallon of water vapor into your home's air. Use your dryer, and make sure it's vented to the outside. Drying clothes on racks inside during a DC summer is a reliable way to push indoor humidity well above the mold-growth threshold.

  • 6

    Seal Basement Walls and Address Crawl Space Moisture

    In DC row homes and older suburban homes, basement walls are a major source of moisture infiltration during summer. Masonry sealers applied to interior basement walls can significantly reduce moisture migration. Crawl spaces should be sealed and vapor-barrier covered ‚Äî an exposed dirt crawl space releases enormous amounts of moisture into the living space above it.

  • 7

    Get Your AC Serviced Before Summer

    An AC system with a dirty evaporator coil, clogged drain line, or low refrigerant doesn't just cool less effectively ‚Äî it dehumidifies less effectively. Annual AC servicing before the DC summer season ensures your system is removing as much moisture from the air as it should.

  • 8

    Monitor with a Hygrometer

    A simple digital hygrometer (available for $10‚Äì15) lets you monitor real-time indoor humidity in different areas of your home. Place one in your basement, one in a bedroom, and one in a main living area. If any area consistently reads above 55%, it needs targeted dehumidification attention.

"A $15 hygrometer and a good dehumidifier are cheaper than mold remediation. The best mold treatment is the one you never need."

What to Do If You Already Have Mold

Humidity control prevents mold from starting ‚Äî but if you already have a mold problem, managing humidity alone won't solve it. Existing mold colonies need to be treated and eliminated before humidity control measures can be effective at preventing regrowth.

If you discover visible mold, notice a persistent musty odor, or experience allergy-like symptoms that improve when you leave home, contact a professional before attempting DIY treatment. Disturbing mold without proper containment spreads spores throughout the building and often makes the problem significantly worse.
