What Is Whole-Building Sanitization?

Whole-building sanitization is a treatment method that decontaminates an entire structure simultaneously ‚Äî treating every surface, every cubic foot of air, and every hidden space in a single application. Unlike spot treatments that address one room or one surface at a time, whole-building approaches are designed to leave no untreated zone where mold spores, bacteria, or pathogens can survive and spread from.

Whole-Building vs. Spot Treatment: Why It Matters

Spot treatments have their place for isolated, minor surface issues. But for recurring mold, post-water-damage cleanup, commercial facilities, or any situation where contamination may have spread beyond a single area, spot treatments consistently fall short. Here's why:

  • Mold spores travel through air. Even if you eliminate a colony in one room, the spores have already spread throughout the building via air movement and HVAC systems.
  • HVAC systems distribute contamination. A mold colony near a return air duct will seed every room the system serves ‚Äî often before the original colony is even noticed.
  • Hidden spaces go untreated. Wall cavities, ceiling plenum spaces, subfloor areas, and attic insulation are impossible to treat with surface methods ‚Äî but dry fog reaches all of them.
  • Partial treatments leave reservoirs. Any untreated pocket of mold or spore contamination is a potential source of regrowth that will recolonize treated areas.
